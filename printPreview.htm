Inspiration for A Midsummer Night's Dream

William Shakespeare wrote A Midsummer Night's Dream between 1590 and 1596. The play takes place in Athens; events revolve around the upcoming wedding of the duke of Athens, Theseus, and Hippolyta.

A Midsummer Night's Dream, A Comedy

The setting for A Midsummer Night's Dream is Athens. At the outset of the play, Theseus has just returned from conquering the Amazons, a race of warrior women. Theseus is about to marry the Amazon queen, Hippolyta. It is just four days before their wedding.

The beginning of the play also introduces four other young lovers: Hermia, Lysander, Demetrius, and Helena.

Deciphering Meaning

At the beginning of the play, Theseus is impatient to get married—he complains that the four days until his nuptials seems like an eternity:

how slow

This old moon wanes! She lingers my desires,

Like to a step-dame or dowager,

Long withering out a young man's revenue.

The reference to the step-dame or dowager is to a step-mother who has been widowed; "Long withering out a young man's revenue" refers to her spending his inheritance. Hippolyta calms Theseus, saying that the time will pass quickly.

The other characters of the play have arrived for the wedding. Egeus has chosen Demetrius as the husband for his daughter Hermia, of whom he says:

As she is mine I may dispose of her;

Which shall be either to this gentleman

Or to her death, according to our law

Immediately provided in that case.

Hermia does not want to marry Demetrius because she is in love with Lysander. But according to the law of Athens, Hermia must follow her father's wishes at the risk of death. Egeus goes to Theseus and asks him to rule on the issue, and Theseus decides she must make her choice between marrying Demetrius or joining a convent by the date of his own wedding. In this way, Shakespeare sets up the timing of events to take place within the next four days.

When Lysander and Hermia are alone, he suggests that they elope. Hermia's friend Helena appears. She is crestfallen because the man she is in love with, Demetrius, is not returning her affection. Demetrius has fallen for Hermia.

These initial events and interactions set the plot of the story in motion.

The Theme of Love

Shakespeare's theme of love being blind or irrational is revealed in several ways in the play. He deliberately makes Lysander and Demetrius similar—almost interchangeable—characters. In some productions, the similarity is reinforced by having actors who look alike. Yet Hermia has fallen hopelessly in love with one of them. In addition, before the play begins we learn that Demetrius favored Helena at one point and suddenly changed his mind and decided to pursue Hermia, although there is almost no rational reason for him to do so.

In a soliloquy at the end of Act I, Scene 1, Helena reflects on the irrationality of love as symbolized by the "blind" Cupid:

Love looks not with the eyes, but with the mind;

And therefore is wing'd Cupid painted blind:

Nor hath Love's mind of any judgement taste;

Wings and no eyes figure unheedy haste:

And therefore is Love said to be a child,

Because in choice he is so oft beguiled.

Even if we pride ourselves on being rational (as Lysander does), there are important elements of love and of ourselves that are beyond our control. "The course of true love never did run smooth," says Lysander.

Shakespeare is known for his excellent use of figurative language and literary devices. In A Midsummer Night's Dream he returns to the same images again and again. For example, his use of the image of the moon is mentioned several times at the beginning of the play and is returned to in other acts as well.

THESEUS

Now, fair Hippolyta, our nuptial hour

Draws on apace; four happy days bring in

Another moon: but, O, methinks, how slow

This old moon wanes! she lingers my desires,

Like to a step-dame or a dowager

Long withering out a young man's revenue.

HIPPOLYTA

Four days will quickly steep themselves in night;

Four nights will quickly dream away the time;

And then the moon, like to a silver bow

New-bent in heaven, shall behold the night

Of our solemnities.

The references to the moon serve several purposes. First, the moon shows the passing of time. It also serves to remind the audience that much of the play will take place at night. During Shakespeare's time, many plays would have taken place during the day and often out of doors, so this reminder helps set the events in context. The moon also helps serve as a reminder to the audience of the dream-like state of the characters and their love.

Shakespeare also reinforces meaning through the use of language. At the outset of the play, for instance, he uses blank verse, unrhymed lines of iambic pentameter. As the mood of the scene shifts, so does the language. Watch the video to learn more.

Poetic Elements of "All the World's a Stage"

Some of the monologues and dialogues in Shakespeare's plays are so powerful that they are almost like poems of their own. "All the World's a Stage," a monologue from As You Like It, is a good example. A monologue is simply a long speech by someone in a play. Like a soliloquy, it may be used to express the characters thoughts, but it can also be spoken to another character in the play. In this case, for instance, Jacques is speaking to Duke Frederick, who has had a change of heart and decided to renounce the world. Jacques says that people's ability to change is fundamental—in fact, people go through many transformations during the course of their lives. He then details seven stages of life.

Let's read the monologue. Click here to read it.

As you can see, the narrator is comparing life to a play—something that the audience is obviously familiar with, since they are attending a play! He says that we merely go through the various stages of our life and recounts seven different stages.

Shakespeare enhances the meaning of this monologue by making it rich with imagery. Through his description, you can see the infant, boy, young man, and old man. He does this not only through visual imagery but by using language to call on other senses as well. You can almost smell the baby who is "mewling and puking in its nurse's arms." In several cases he calls on our sense of hearing as well: the lover sings "a woeful ballad"; the soldier is "full of strange oaths." Let's look at his description of the "sixth age," which is rich in imagery:

The sixth age shifts

Into the lean and slippered pantaloon,

With spectacles on nose and pouch on side;

His youthful hose, well saved, a world too wide

For his shrunk shank, and his big manly voice,

Turning again toward childish treble, pipes

And whistles in his sound.

Arousing the audience's senses in this way makes the end of the monologue even more startling when he describes the last stage of men:

mere oblivion,

Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans everything.

Structure and Style

Like the first act of A Midsummer Night's Dream, the "All the World's a Stage" monologue is written in iambic pentameter.

Shakespeare also pays close attention to how the words sound as they are spoken aloud. The use of poetic elements such as alliteration, assonance, and consonance help set the cadence and tone of the work. Remember, this monologue is heard, not read. Sometimes listening to it aloud can help you understand elements such as meter and how the words sound. As you listen, pay particular attention to the use of alliteration, assonance, and consonance.

Let's look again at the first part of the monologue.

All the world's a stage,

And all the men and women merely players;

They have their exits and their entrances,

And one man in his time plays many parts,

His acts being seven ages. At first, the infant,

Mewling and puking in the nurse's arms.

“Men” and “women” have the same ending sounds—an example of consonance. Exits and entrances have the same beginning sounds—an example of alliteration. Mewling and puking have similar internal vowel sounds—an example of assonance.

You can also see in just this one part of the monologue how the regular rhythm and repetition makes the language seem more lyrical and varied. He talks of men and women, exits and entrances, mewling and puking—writing in pairs rather than in groups of three. At the very end of the monologue, another technique is used: repetition.

Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans everything.

The repetition of the word sans (meaning without) reinforces one of the poem's main themes—the sad fact that in the last stage of our life, we lose everything.
